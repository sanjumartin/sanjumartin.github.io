# Performance Measurement & Attribution: A Primer

**Category:** Performance | **Date:** June 10, 2026 | **Read time:** 5 min

---

Performance measurement is the foundation upon which investment management accountability is built. After years of calculating, verifying, and explaining performance figures across major asset managers, I want to share a clear primer on what these concepts mean and why they matter.

## What Is Performance Measurement?

Performance measurement quantifies the return generated by an investment portfolio over a defined period. The goal is to answer: *Did the portfolio make money, how much, and relative to what?*

The two primary return types used in institutional investment management are:

- **Time-Weighted Return (TWR)** — eliminates the effect of external cash flows, making it the standard for evaluating manager skill
- **Money-Weighted Return (MWR / IRR)** — reflects the investor's actual experience, including the timing and size of cash flows

GIPS mandates TWR for most composites, though MWR is now permitted for certain private equity structures.

## What Is Attribution Analysis?

Attribution analysis answers *why* a portfolio delivered its return. It decomposes the difference between portfolio return and benchmark return (the "active return" or "alpha") into sources.

The most widely used framework is the **Brinson-Hood-Beebower (BHB) model**, which breaks active return into:

### 1. Allocation Effect
Did the manager overweight or underweight sectors/countries that outperformed?

*Allocation Effect = (Portfolio Weight − Benchmark Weight) × (Benchmark Sector Return − Total Benchmark Return)*

### 2. Selection Effect
Did the manager pick better securities within each sector/country than the benchmark held?

*Selection Effect = Benchmark Weight × (Portfolio Sector Return − Benchmark Sector Return)*

### 3. Interaction Effect
The combined effect of allocation and selection decisions (sometimes absorbed into the selection effect).

## Why This Matters for Clients

When institutional clients evaluate investment managers, they want to understand:

1. **Consistency** — is the alpha repeatable or luck?
2. **Source** — is the manager adding value through smart sector bets, stock picking, or both?
3. **Risk-adjusted** — are the returns worth the tracking error taken?

At Mercer and AXA IM, producing accurate attribution reports that answered these questions was central to the client reporting function. A well-constructed attribution report builds trust and justifies mandate retention.

## Common Measurement Errors to Avoid

- **Linking sub-period returns additively** instead of geometrically — this distorts multi-period analysis
- **Cash drag** — not accounting for uninvested cash in the benchmark comparison
- **Currency effects** — failing to separate local return from currency contribution in global portfolios
- **Incorrect composite construction** — including non-discretionary accounts that distort the strategy's true performance

## The Role of Technology

Modern platforms like Stat-Pro Global, FactSet, and Morningstar Direct automate much of the calculation. However, understanding the underlying methodology is essential — systems can automate correct processes, but they can also automate errors at scale.

The best analysts I have worked with treat these tools as calculation engines, not black boxes. They validate results, investigate anomalies, and can reconstruct calculations manually when needed.
