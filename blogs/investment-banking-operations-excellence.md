# Operational Excellence in Investment Banking: Lessons from 14 Years

**Category:** Operations | **Date:** June 20, 2026 | **Read time:** 6 min

---

Fourteen years across BNY Mellon, BNP Paribas, AXA Investment Managers, and Mercer have taught me that operational excellence in investment banking is rarely about dramatic breakthroughs — it is built through disciplined, incremental improvements in process, people, and data quality.

Here are the most important lessons I have carried with me.

## 1. Data Quality Is Everything

In investment operations, a single data error can cascade into incorrect performance figures, wrong client reports, and regulatory compliance failures. I have seen how a small pricing error in one account can invalidate an entire composite's history.

**What works:**
- Daily reconciliation checks at the source, before downstream processing
- Dual-control for critical data inputs (benchmarks, AUM, cash flows)
- Automated exception alerts rather than manual sampling
- Clear ownership — every data element should have one accountable person

## 2. Standard Operating Procedures Are Not Bureaucracy

When I joined BNY Mellon early in my career, I was surprised by the weight placed on written SOPs. Later, when we successfully migrated processes from the UK Leeds office, I understood why: documented procedures are the only reliable way to transfer complex operations across geographies and teams.

A good SOP:
- Describes the *why*, not just the *how*
- Includes escalation paths for exceptions
- Is reviewed and updated quarterly, not just when something breaks
- Is written by the people who do the work, validated by those who audit it

## 3. SLA Management Is a Leadership Skill

Meeting Service Level Agreements in operations is not just about fast processing — it requires understanding capacity, prioritising work intelligently, communicating proactively with stakeholders, and making real-time decisions when volumes spike.

At AXA IM, I managed SLAs across a team of 11, including multiple team leads. The single most effective practice was a daily morning pulse call — 15 minutes, all team leads, to surface any risk to the day's deliverables before the client noticed.

*Prevention always costs less than remediation.*

## 4. Lean Principles Apply Directly to Financial Operations

I completed Lean Six Sigma training and led a Lean Green Belt project at AXA IM. The tools — value stream mapping, root cause analysis, FMEA — translate directly into investment operations.

Key applications:
- **Value stream mapping** to identify handoff delays between onshore and offshore teams
- **SIPOC reviews** to evaluate process boundaries every quarter (used at BNP Paribas)
- **Kaizen events** for rapid process improvement (the Kaizen Wave Idea at BNY Mellon saved 0.75 FTE)
- **Error analysis meetings** for structured root-cause review after incidents

## 5. Offshore/Onshore Transitions Require Trust, Not Just Process

Two of the most complex assignments in my career involved migrating GIPS and QO activities from AXA IM Paris (in 2015 and 2019). The technical process migration was the easier part. Building the working relationship between Paris and Pune teams — across languages, time zones, and work cultures — was the real challenge.

What made it work:
- Extended knowledge transfer periods (never rush a migration)
- Shadowing before solo processing
- Open escalation culture — offshore team must feel safe raising issues to onshore without fear
- Shared KPIs so both teams were measured on the same outcomes

## 6. Your Team Is the Process

The most resilient operations teams I have led share one characteristic: every member understands the end goal, not just their individual step. When people understand *why* their task matters — who the client is, how their output is used — their accuracy and ownership improve.

Invest in your team's understanding, not just their speed.
